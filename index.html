Swarm Intelligence Research

In this project, I investigated the use of agent-based models to enhance collective decision-making and resource allocation in multi-agent systems. Building on the concept of swarm robotics, I integrated Dihedral and Cyclic group theory approaches into an **evolutionary algorithm** to discover optimal coordination strategies. These strategies improved collision avoidance, task distribution, and formation control across numerous simulations (like the Halite Challenge), demonstrating that symmetry-based approaches can significantly boost performance in real-world robotics and complex systems. The research culminated in a paper showcasing how algebraic analysis and geometric optimization can shape more robust, scalable, and energy-efficient swarms.

Startup Success Prediction Model

This comprehensive study analyzed **4,000+ startup cases** to understand which factors drive entrepreneurial success. I compiled a robust dataset encompassing variables such as industry sector, company age, prior founder experience, educational background, and geographic region. Leveraging advanced machine learning (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, Logistic Regression), I identified the key predictors—like Years Since Founded and Number of Founded Organizations—that have the highest impact on a startup’s success rate. Verdant - Prouce for All

Verdant connects community gardens to shelters in need, helping them plan, plant, and harvest produce efficiently. Shelters can submit requests through the platform, which uses machine learning to determine a planting schedule that we as volunteers can follow to provide as much as possible. I built Verdant around a neural network architecture designed to forecast planting schedules and expected produce yields. Specifically, I fine-tuned a pretrained LSTM-based model (originally from Keras/TensorFlow’s time-series forecasting repository) using historical weather data (e.g., from local NOAA data feeds), soil-condition metrics, and past yield logs collected from regional community gardens. The neural network’s core layers comprise several LSTM blocks for sequence modeling and a final dense layer to output precise harvest predictions. This training process enabled Verdant to factor in weather fluctuations, seasonal soil dynamics, and historical donation patterns. By integrating these data-driven forecasts into a user-friendly web interface, the app empowers local gardeners to plan crop rotations more accurately, minimize waste, and optimize produce donations to nearby food banks.

GitHub Repo: https://github.com/akshajnad/GardenApp

911 Call Analysis Model

This Flask-based application leverages **association rule mining** (Apriori) on 911 call data to unearth hidden patterns and frequent itemsets regarding call reasons (EMS, Fire, Traffic) and other attributes. I engineered an end-to-end pipeline where data is ingested, cleaned, and transformed into a one-hot encoded format, then processed by the Apriori algorithm to derive data-driven insights. These insights can guide emergency response teams on how to allocate resources more effectively. The app features a sleek web interface for real-time exploration, letting users quickly see top rules and filter calls by reason.

CT Science and Engineering Fair: Optimizing Magnetic Fusion Reactors

For the Connecticut Science and Engineering Fair (CSEF), I developed a theoretical optimization tool, dubbed “TOAST” (Theoretical Optimization Algorithm for Sustainable Technology), that models **Magneto-Inertial Fusion** configurations. Drawing upon the Grad-Shafranov equation, I introduced a customizable pressure function J(ψ) = α·ψ - β·ψ² + sin(ψ) + 0.1·cos(ψ) to capture nuanced plasma behavior inside a Field Reversed Configuration (FRC). By varying α (linear pressure gradient), β (ratio of plasma to magnetic pressure), and the FRC radius, I simulated how changes in magnetic flux profiles affect plasma temperature and density under the **Lawson Criterion**.

Through a combination of penalty-based optimization and advanced numerical methods (including `solve_bvp` in Python), I discovered a sweet spot (α ≈ 102.4, β ≈ 22.9, radius ≈ 13.6 m) that balances temperature (reaching ~125 million Kelvin) and density without exceeding engineering limits (150 million Kelvin, 1022 particles/m³). The tool also explored the interplay of additional factors like electrical current and pulse duration, highlighting how these can be tuned to maximize net energy yield. This project offers a conceptual framework for future fusion research and underlines the potential of computational modeling in accelerating clean energy innovations.

GitHub Repo: https://github.com/akshajnad/CSEF
